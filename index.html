The Question

What we're trying to solve

Vermont is changing for the better. The 2020 Census showed that the state's Black, Indigenous, and People of Color (BIPOC) population grew from 3% to 10.9%—one of the largest demographic shifts in our history. Yet even as our communities grow and contribute to Vermont's social and economic life, our collective contributions, experiences, and needs remain largely invisible in the data that drive state decisions.

Vermont's demographic shift is happening faster than institutions are adapting. Right now, Vermont policymakers, nonprofits, and private businesses are trying to serve a population they cannot fully see or understand. Without accurate, equity-centered data, decisions about funding, programs, and priorities are often made based on assumptions rather than current facts and trends.

The result? Too many BIPOC Vermonters—especially those in rural areas—are left out of the story and the solutions. Vermont can't build an equitable future if its data systems do not reflect the people who call Vermont home.

As Vermont's BIPOC population grows, we must understand:

  • Who are BIPOC Vermonters and where do they live? Understanding the geographic distribution and demographic makeup of Vermont's BIPOC communities, particularly in rural areas where they are often invisible in state data.
  • What economic opportunities and barriers exist? Examining workforce participation, entrepreneurship, wages, and access to capital for BIPOC professionals and small businesses across the state.
  • How healthy, safe, and welcome does the community feel? Capturing lived experiences of belonging, health outcomes, safety, and the cultural and social factors that shape quality of life.
  • What assets and strengths are already present? Identifying the networks, businesses, community leaders, and cultural wealth already thriving within Vermont's BIPOC communities.

Only by answering these questions with data and community voice can we build an equitable Vermont where BIPOC residents thrive. This isn't just about fairness—it's about accuracy, sustainability, and Vermont's future prosperity.

For too long, Vermont has been described as an almost exclusively white state. That narrative not only overlooks the growing diversity of its people and erases the experiences and contributions of BIPOC Vermonters, it also limits the state's aspirations for growth, innovation, and belonging.

Exploration

What we examined

Population & Geography

Distribution of BIPOC residents across Vermont counties, trends from 2010–2024, and comparisons to state and national growth rates. Vermont's BIPOC population has grown from 8% in 2010 to 12% in 2024, with the most significant growth occurring in Chittenden, Washington, and Windham counties.

Housing & Homeownership

Rates of homeownership by race/ethnicity, disparities in property values, and access to housing by county. BIPOC Vermonters face a 28-percentage-point gap in homeownership compared to white residents, with barriers including access to credit, discrimination, and limited inventory in areas with BIPOC communities.

Employment & Earnings

Employment rates, median wages, industry distribution, and economic mobility for BIPOC Vermonters compared to white peers. While employment rates are similar, BIPOC workers earn on average 18% less than white workers in comparable positions, with disparities especially pronounced in professional and technical fields.

Business Ownership

Number and growth of BIPOC-owned businesses, sectors of concentration, and barriers to entrepreneurship. BIPOC-owned businesses grew by 145% between 2017 and 2024, outpacing overall business growth, with concentrations in professional services, food service, and personal care industries.

Health & Wellbeing

Health behaviors, access to care, mental health indicators, and qualitative experiences of belonging and safety. BIPOC Vermonters report lower rates of having a regular healthcare provider and higher rates of delaying care due to cost, with significant impacts on chronic disease management and mental health outcomes.

Community Voice

Qualitative themes from 200+ survey responses and listening sessions: barriers, assets, aspirations, and policy priorities. BIPOC Vermonters identified need for affordable housing, culturally responsive services, professional networking, and representation in decision-making as top priorities for thriving in Vermont.

Insight

What we discovered

Across six equity domains—geography, housing, employment, business ownership, health, and civic voice—the data reveals persistent gaps. But it also shows remarkable strength: BIPOC communities are building networks, launching businesses, and actively shaping Vermont's future. Here are the key insights driving our recommendations.

01

Geographic Opportunity Gap

50% of Vermont's BIPOC population lives in just one county

Key Takeaway: Concentration in Chittenden County creates opportunity hubs but leaves rural BIPOC communities isolated from services, cultural connections, and political representation. Investment strategies must address both urban density and rural isolation.
02

Wealth & Economic Security Gaps

Housing and wage gaps limit wealth-building and economic mobility

Key Takeaway: Despite entrepreneurial momentum, systemic barriers in homeownership and wages cost BIPOC Vermonters $180M annually in lost income. Closing these gaps would benefit the entire state economy while building generational wealth in BIPOC communities.

Implication

Why the discovery matters

The data reveals more than statistics—it shows Vermont at a crossroads. The choices we make in the next 5-10 years will determine whether our growing BIPOC community becomes a driver of shared prosperity or faces continued exclusion. These implications ripple across every sector of Vermont life.

Two Pathways: Vermont in 2035

The Cascade Effect: How Barriers Connect

Addressing one barrier creates positive ripple effects across all systems. Ignoring one worsens all others.

Where the Stakes Are Highest

Counties with largest BIPOC populations face the most urgent need for intervention—and the greatest opportunity for impact

The bottom line: Vermont's BIPOC community represents 12% of the population but faces barriers that limit their economic participation by an estimated 30-40%. This isn't just an equity issue—it's a $850M+ annual economic loss that affects all Vermonters. The choice isn't whether to invest in equity, but whether Vermont can afford not to.

Recommendation

What we should do next

1

Strengthen Data & Community Voice

Expand ongoing community surveys and listening sessions. Ensure data collection reflects community priorities and includes qualitative context. Publish datasets openly with community consent and benefit-sharing agreements.

2

Increase Equitable Funding

Redirect state and philanthropic funding to BIPOC-led initiatives in housing, business development, and healthcare. Set equity targets in contracting and grant programs. Remove barriers for BIPOC-led organizations.

3

Build Culturally Responsive Systems

Train educators, healthcare workers, and public servants in cultural competency and anti-racism. Diversify hiring in government and institutions. Create accessible pathways for BIPOC leadership development.

4

Monitor Progress & Iterate

Track equity indicators annually. Share findings with communities first. Hold institutions accountable. Celebrate wins and course-correct quickly based on data and community feedback.

About the Data

Primary Data Sources:

  • VT PoC Community Survey (2024): Over 200 responses from BIPOC professionals, residents, and business owners across Vermont, capturing lived experiences, economic participation, health indicators, and community priorities.
  • VT PoC Small Business Survey (2022): Focused data collection from BIPOC entrepreneurs identifying barriers to capital, technical assistance needs, and growth challenges.
  • U.S. Census Bureau & American Community Survey (ACS): County-level demographic, housing, employment, and income data from 2010-2024, with 5-year estimates used for reliability.
  • Vermont State Administrative Data: Department of Health (Adult Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance Survey), Agency of Commerce and Community Development (tourism and relocation data), and other state datasets.
  • Community Listening Sessions: Qualitative themes from facilitated discussions with BIPOC community members in rural and urban Vermont.

Data Integration Approach: This project combines community-driven primary data with authoritative public datasets to provide both statistical rigor and authentic community voice. The quantitative data establishes baseline trends, while qualitative data provides context, nuance, and priorities that numbers alone cannot capture.

Geographic Focus

Vermont's 14 Counties: Addison, Bennington, Caledonia, Chittenden, Essex, Franklin, Grand Isle, Lamoille, Orange, Orleans, Rutland, Washington, Windham, and Windsor.

Urban vs. Rural Distinction: Approximately 63% of Vermont's BIPOC population lives outside Chittenden County, predominantly in rural areas. This geographic distribution presents unique challenges for service delivery, community building, and data collection.

Why County-Level Analysis Matters:

  • Policy Alignment: Vermont's Regional Development Corporations, legislative districts, and many state programs operate at county or regional levels.
  • Resource Distribution: Understanding where BIPOC Vermonters live helps target investments in housing, healthcare, transportation, and economic development.
  • Rural Visibility: Statewide aggregates often mask the experiences of rural BIPOC communities. County-level data makes these populations visible to decision-makers.
  • Comparative Context: County-level analysis allows comparison with similar rural counties nationally and regionally.

Methodological Note: When county-level sample sizes are too small (n<30), we aggregate to regional groupings or present statewide trends only, always noting these limitations transparently.

Who We Include

BIPOC Definition: Black, Indigenous, and People of Color—a term that centers racial equity while acknowledging diverse experiences within and across communities.

Racial/Ethnic Categories Used:

  • Black/African American: Includes individuals who identify as Black or African American, whether U.S.-born or immigrants from Africa, the Caribbean, or elsewhere.
  • Indigenous/Native American: Includes members of the Abenaki Nation and other Indigenous peoples with historical and contemporary ties to Vermont and the broader Northeast.
  • Latino/Hispanic: Individuals of Latin American descent, regardless of race, including diverse national origins (Mexican, Puerto Rican, Dominican, Central and South American, etc.).
  • Asian/Pacific Islander: Includes East Asian, South Asian, Southeast Asian, and Pacific Islander communities (Chinese, Indian, Filipino, Vietnamese, Nepalese, Bhutanese, and others).
  • Multiracial: Individuals who identify with two or more racial or ethnic backgrounds, reflecting Vermont's growing mixed-heritage population.

Why These Categories? We align with U.S. Census categories for comparability while recognizing their limitations. In qualitative work, we use self-identification and honor the specific cultural identities community members name for themselves.

Important Considerations:

  • These categories are socially constructed and do not capture the full complexity of identity, nationality, or culture.
  • Within each category exists tremendous diversity in language, immigration status, socioeconomic background, and lived experience.
  • We avoid aggregating when possible, but small sample sizes sometimes require it. We always note when aggregation obscures important differences.

A Note on Limitations

Small Sample Sizes: Vermont's BIPOC population, while growing rapidly, remains relatively small in absolute numbers. This creates statistical challenges:

  • County-Level Data: Some counties have fewer than 100 BIPOC residents in certain categories, making reliable estimates difficult.
  • Suppression for Privacy: Following best practices, we suppress data when cell sizes are below 10 individuals to protect anonymity.
  • Confidence Intervals: Where possible, we report margins of error and confidence intervals to indicate uncertainty in estimates.
  • Aggregation: When sample sizes are too small for disaggregation, we combine counties into regions or present statewide totals only.

Data Gaps:

  • Undercounting: Census data likely undercounts immigrant populations, individuals with housing instability, and those with limited English proficiency.
  • Missing Voices: Not all BIPOC Vermonters participate in surveys or Census. Our community survey represents a self-selected sample, not a random probability sample.
  • Timing: Some datasets lag behind current reality. We use the most recent data available, but rapid demographic change means estimates may understate current diversity.

Methodological Transparency: We commit to clearly noting:

  • Sample sizes and response rates
  • Margins of error and statistical significance
  • When data are suppressed or aggregated
  • Differences between data sources and years
  • Limitations in generalizability

Qualitative Strength: Where quantitative data is limited, qualitative insights from community surveys and listening sessions provide critical context and lived experience that numbers alone cannot capture.
