While still early in the nation's demographic transition, Vermont is experiencing significant growth in its Black, Indigenous, and People of Color communities. This growth carries implications for our economy, culture, and shared future.

The Question

What we're trying to solve

As Vermont's BIPOC population grows, we must understand:

  • Who are BIPOC Vermonters and where do they live?
  • What economic opportunities and barriers exist?
  • How healthy, safe, and welcome does the community feel?
  • What assets and strengths are already present?

Only by answering these questions with data and community voice can we build an equitable Vermont where BIPOC residents thrive.

Context

Background needed to understand the data

About the Data

This analysis combines 2024 VT PoC Community Survey responses, U.S. Census and American Community Survey data, Vermont state administrative datasets, and community listening sessions.

Geographic Focus

We examine state-level trends and county-level variations across Vermont's 14 counties, with special attention to rural vs. urban and Chittenden County patterns.

Who We Include

Our BIPOC categories include Black/African American, Indigenous, Latino/Hispanic, Asian/Pacific Islander, and multi-racial Vermonters, aligned with Census definitions where applicable.

A Note on Limitations

Small population counts in some counties require aggregation. We highlight confidence intervals and suppress data where samples are too small (n<10) to protect privacy.

Insight

What we discovered

Growth Concentrates in Chittenden County

Nearly 50% of Vermont's BIPOC population lives in Chittenden County. While this reflects urban economic opportunities, it also means many rural counties have limited BIPOC representation, affecting service access and community visibility.

Homeownership Gaps Persist

BIPOC Vermonters own homes at rates 15‚Äì20 percentage points below white Vermonters. Barriers include down payment gaps, credit access, and historical discrimination in lending.

Wage Disparities Across Sectors

BIPOC workers earn 10‚Äì15% less than white counterparts in similar roles. Healthcare, education, and technology sectors show larger gaps than other industries.

Strong Entrepreneurial Energy

Despite barriers, BIPOC-owned businesses grew 35% over the past five years‚Äîfaster than the state average. Restaurants, professional services, and retail dominate.

Health Disparities Reflect Systemic Issues

BIPOC communities report higher rates of chronic disease, mental health challenges, and barriers to quality care. Trust in medical systems remains low due to historical trauma.

"We're Here. We're Building."

From survey respondents: Despite barriers, community members express pride, resilience, and commitment to building Vermont together. Assets like family networks, cultural traditions, and mutual aid are critical strengths.

Recommendation

What we should do next

1

Strengthen Data & Community Voice

Expand ongoing community surveys and listening sessions. Ensure data collection reflects community priorities and includes qualitative context. Publish datasets openly with community consent and benefit-sharing agreements.

2

Increase Equitable Funding

Redirect state and philanthropic funding to BIPOC-led initiatives in housing, business development, and healthcare. Set equity targets in contracting and grant programs. Remove barriers for BIPOC-led organizations.

3

Build Culturally Responsive Systems

Train educators, healthcare workers, and public servants in cultural competency and anti-racism. Diversify hiring in government and institutions. Create accessible pathways for BIPOC leadership development.

4

Monitor Progress & Iterate

Track equity indicators annually. Share findings with communities first. Hold institutions accountable. Celebrate wins and course-correct quickly based on data and community feedback.
